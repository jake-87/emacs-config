#+title:Cosine rule, algebra


For vectors, the cosine rule states that
$|b - a|^2 = |a| + |b| - 2|a||b|\cos\theta$.

Equivalently:

$|b - a|^2 = |a| + |b| - 2|a \cdot b|$.

This can be proven from the expansion of $|b-a|^2$.


This can also be proven geometrically, using the Pythagoras theorem.

\[|AB|^2 = |BP|^2 + |AP|^2\]

Imagine a triangle:

          A
   
O       B P

\[|BP| = ||OP| - |OB|| = ||a|\cos\theta - |b||\]

\[|AP| = |a|\sin\theta\]

\begin{align*}
|AB|^2 &= |a|^2\cos^2\theta - 2|a||b|\cos\theta + |b|^2 + |a|^2\sin^2\theta
\\
&= |a|^2(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta) + |b|^2 - 2|a||b|\cos\theta
\\
&= |a|^2 + |b|^2 - 2|a||b|\cos\theta
\end{align*}
